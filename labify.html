Overview

Labify is a concept mobile product designed to improve access to preventive healthcare by enabling users to order and schedule at-home blood tests.

The product focuses on reducing friction in the blood testing process by allowing users to select tests, schedule home visits, and track results in one seamless experience.

My role

Product Designer (end-to-end)
Research, UX, UI, product strategy & decision-making

Why I started this project?

I initiated this project to explore how digital products can improve access to healthcare services, particularly for people with limited time or mobility.

Traditional blood testing processes are often fragmented, time-consuming, and poorly adapted to users’ everyday lives. This project allowed me to define the problem space, validate key user needs, and make product decisions balancing convenience, trust, and medical reliability.

Problem Statement and Possible Solution

Problem

The current process of ordering and conducting blood tests is often inconvenient and time-consuming, requiring patients to visit clinics or laboratories and endure long wait times. This creates a significant barrier to healthcare access and monitoring for many individuals. There is a need for a more convenient and accessible solution that enables patients to order and track blood tests from the comfort of their homes.
The main challenge was not only access, but also building trust in a remote medical service.

Solution

A user-friendly and accessible mobile application that allows patients to easily order and track blood tests from home. The app features an intuitive interface that guides users through selecting the tests they need, scheduling a specialist visit for blood collection, and receiving their results.
The solution prioritizes clarity, transparency, and guidance at each step to reduce uncertainty and increase user confidence.

Research

Research approach

To better understand user needs and constraints around home blood testing, I conducted qualitative interviews with 9 participants and a quantitative survey with 15 respondents.
The goal was to identify the main barriers, decision factors, and trust concerns related to ordering medical tests outside of traditional clinics.

Key insights

  • Time, not price, is the main barrier – users are willing to pay more to avoid long waiting times and travel.
  • Trust is a critical adoption factor – users are concerned about professionalism, data privacy, and medical reliability.
  • Most users already avoid public clinics, which indicates readiness for private, at-home solutions.
  • Home testing is perceived as a convenience upgrade, not a replacement for medical quality.

These insights directly informed the scope and priorities of the initial product version.

Product implications & decisions

Based on the research, I made the following product decisions:

  • Prioritized fast and flexible scheduling over advanced personalization features.
  • Designed the onboarding to build trust early, focusing on clarity, transparency, and credentials of medical staff.
  • Made test history and results access a core feature, not an add-on.
  • Limited the initial scope to a small, well-explained set of tests to reduce cognitive load and uncertainty.
  • Chose in-app communication as the primary channel, as users strongly preferred app-based interactions over phone calls.
I intentionally deprioritized advanced personalization and recommendation features to focus on trust, clarity, and core functionality in the first release.

User Persona

This persona represents the primary user segment identified during research and was used to guide key product decisions.
Anna represents users who value convenience and time efficiency but require a high level of trust and medical reliability before adopting a home testing service.

Based on Anna’s needs and constraints, I made the following decisions:

  • Prioritized fast scheduling and minimal steps over feature richness.
  • Designed onboarding to explicitly communicate medical credibility and data security.
  • Ensured test history access is always visible and easy to find.
  • Avoided complex medical language in favor of clear, reassuring copy.
  • Designed flows that allow ordering tests for family members, not only for oneself.

Empathy Map

This empathy map synthesizes emotional and behavioral insights that directly informed the problem definition and core product priorities.

Key takeaways from empathy mapping:

  • Emotional friction (anxiety and uncertainty) is as important as functional friction (time and travel).
  • Trust concerns extend beyond test accuracy to the entire service experience.
  • Waiting and unpredictability are stronger pain points than price.
  • Users actively seek social proof before committing to medical services.
  • Clear guidance and reassurance are required throughout the flow.

These insights directly shaped the problem definition and initial product scope.

User Journey Map

This journey map visualizes the current-state experience of a traditional blood test and highlights the key pain points the product aims to eliminate.

Based on the journey analysis, the following opportunities were identified:

  • Eliminate physical waiting time by enabling at-home blood collection.
  • Reduce uncertainty by providing clear scheduling and real-time status updates.
  • Remove the need for repeated visits by making results accessible digitally.
  • Support time-constrained users by allowing flexible date selection.
  • Extend value by enabling test ordering for family members.

These opportunities directly informed the definition of the core product flow and MVP scope.

User Flow

This user flow represents the core MVP experience focused on fast test ordering, minimal steps, and early trust-building.

Key decisions behind this flow:

  • Centralized all primary actions on the home screen to support quick decision-making.
  • Designed multiple entry points to test selection (search, categories, sets) to match different user behaviors.
  • Included patient selection early to support ordering tests for family members.
  • Minimized the checkout process to essential steps only.
  • Prioritized date selection after test choice to align with user expectations.

This flow directly informed the wireframes and interaction design of the MVP screens.

Information architecture & navigation decisions

Card sorting was used as a supporting method to validate category groupings and navigation labels.

The information architecture was designed to support fast decision-making, reduce cognitive load, and align with users’ mental models identified during research.

Key decisions behind the information architecture:

  • Grouped tests under a single Tests section to avoid decision paralysis.
  • Separated Cart as a standalone area to clearly distinguish selection from checkout.
  • Placed Test history under Profile to align with users’ expectations around personal medical data.
  • Prioritized Search and Categories to support different discovery behaviors.
  • Limited top-level navigation to reduce cognitive load for first-time users.
  • Ensured critical actions are reachable within two taps from the home screen.

Core screens (pre-validation)

These high-fidelity wireframes represent the first complete version of the MVP used for usability testing and feedback validation. The goal at this stage was not visual polish, but validating structure, flow clarity, and trust-related content before moving into visual design.

Validation & iteration

Screens were validated through lightweight usability testing to identify friction points in test discovery, selection, and decision-making. Based on the findings, the interface was iterated to improve clarity, reduce cognitive load, and strengthen trust-related signals.

Test sets (home screen)

Insight: Users struggled to understand what differentiates test sets at a glance.
Decision: Added short descriptions to clarify purpose and intended use.
Outcome: Faster comparison and more confident selection.

Test selection list

Insight: A long list of individual tests felt overwhelming and hard to scan.
Decision: Grouped tests and improved visual hierarchy.
Outcome: Reduced cognitive load and improved scannability.

Product card (test set)

Insight: Users needed more reassurance before committing to a test set.
Decision: Expanded the product card with detailed information, preparation guidance, and related tests.
Outcome: Increased trust and perceived medical credibility.

Design system foundations

To ensure consistency, scalability, and trust across the product, I defined a set of foundational design principles covering color, typography, and core UI elements.
The visual system was designed to reinforce medical credibility, support readability, and remain flexible for future feature expansion.

Visual design principles

Key decisions behind the information architecture:

  • Clarity over decoration – interfaces prioritize readability and task completion
  • Trust through consistency – predictable layouts and restrained visuals reduce uncertainty.
  • Scalable simplicity – components and layouts are designed to accommodate future features.

Outcome & learnings

What this project demonstrates

This project demonstrates my ability to lead an end-to-end product design process - from problem discovery and user research to defining MVP scope, validating assumptions through usability testing, and iterating toward a scalable solution.

Key outcomes

As a result of the design and validation process:

  • A clear MVP experience was defined, focused on fast test ordering, minimal friction, and early trust-building.
  • Key decision points in test selection and checkout were simplified to reduce cognitive load.
  • Trust and medical credibility were reinforced through information hierarchy, content clarity, and visual consistency.
  • The product was designed to support ordering tests for both individual users and family members.

What I would do next

If I were to continue developing this product, I would explore restructuring the booking flow by allowing users to secure an appointment first and then select tests afterward. This approach could reduce decision pressure, better align with time-constrained users’ mental models, and potentially increase conversion in the early stages of the journey.
